The Connection Between Wildfires and Climate Change: A Growing Global Crisis

Published: January 19, 2025 By Nathani Baesso

In recent years, wildfires have become a devastating symbol of the climate crisis, igniting not only forests but also urgent conversations about our planet’s future. Research consistently shows a direct link between climate change and the increased frequency, intensity, and extent of wildfires.

Rising global temperatures, longer periods of drought, and a drier atmosphere have created the perfect conditions for fires to spark and spread uncontrollably across vast regions.

Firefighters are currently battling a series of wildfires that erupted earlier in 2025 in Los Angeles County, California. Driven by a fierce windstorm, the fires have burnt over 17,000 acres, destroyed at least 1,300 structures, and forced the evacuation of more than 179,000 residents.

The Palisades and Eaton fires have wreaked havoc in affluent neighborhoods like Pacific Palisades and northern valley communities such as Altadena. As President Biden declared the situation "catastrophic," federal resources—including 400 firefighters and 30 planes—have been deployed to assist in managing the crisis.

This article explores how climate change accelerates the wildfire crisis, the ripple effects of deforestation in the Amazon, and the urgent need for global action to mitigate these disasters.

It’s not about preventing the next wildfire—it’s about addressing the climate emergency that makes them inevitable.

What Are Wildfires?

Wildfires are large, uncontrolled fires that spread rapidly through natural vegetation, and they can cause extensive damage to ecosystems, homes, and human lives.

They occur across the globe and impact a variety of landscapes, including forests, grasslands, and savannas.

How Do Wildfires Start?

Wildfires can ignite from both natural and human-induced causes. One of the most common natural triggers is lightning: the intense heat from a lightning strike can dry out a tree’s moisture and spark flames, leading to a fire.

Human activity can also play a significant role in starting wildfires. Discarded cigarette butts, unattended campfires, and sparks from faulty power lines are among the leading causes.

Once a fire ignites, certain conditions—such as prolonged droughts, high temperatures, and strong, dry winds—can exacerbate its spread. As the fire burns, it generates intense heat that dries out nearby vegetation, creating a chain reaction that fuels the blaze.

The Impact of Wildfires

Wildfires have far-reaching consequences for the environment, wildlife, and human populations, such as:

  1. Air Pollution: Wildfires release smoke and harmful pollutants into the atmosphere, which can lead to respiratory problems and other health issues for nearby communities – and potentially not only nearby.

  2. Greenhouse Gas Emissions: The fires increase atmospheric levels of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases, fueling climate change even further.

  3. Water Contamination: Ash and debris from wildfires can pollute water supplies, which affects drinking water quality as well as aquatic ecosystems.

  4. Wildlife Endangerment: Fires destroy habitats, forcing animals to flee or, often, simply perish.

  5. Soil Erosion: Vegetation loss caused by fires increases soil erosion, disrupting ecosystems and making it even harder for plant life to regenerate.

These phenomena are not simply natural disasters. They are interwoven with human activity and climate change, making their prevention and management critical for the health of the planet.

How Climate Change Increases the Occurrence of Wildfires

Climate change is closely linked to the increase in the frequency, intensity, and duration of wildfires. Rising global temperatures, prolonged droughts, changing rainfall patterns, and shifting ecosystems create conditions that make wildfires both more frequent and more destructive.

Increased Temperatures

Since the late 19th century, global temperatures have risen by approximately 1°C (1.7°F). This change, while seemingly small, has dried out vegetation, making it more flammable.

Higher temperatures also extend the fire season, providing more opportunities for wildfires to ignite and spread.

Prolonged Droughts

Changes in climate have intensified drought conditions around the world, drying out forests and grasslands. This creates ideal conditions for wildfires, as dry vegetation serves as highly combustible fuel.

Altered Rainfall Patterns

Shifts in rainfall patterns can lead to heavy vegetation growth during wetter periods, which then dries out during the hotter seasons, thereby providing more fuel for fires.

Additionally, post-fire landscapes are more prone to erosion and landslides during subsequent heavy rains, further worsening this vicious cycle.

Longer Fire Seasons

Warmer temperatures and earlier snowmelt extend fire seasons significantly. On average, fire seasons have lengthened by 27% globally, particularly in fire-prone regions like the Amazon, Mediterranean countries, and western North America.

More Lightning Strikes

Rising global temperatures increase atmospheric moisture, leading to more thunderstorms and more lightning.

Scientists estimate that lightning strikes increase by approximately 12% for every 1°C rise in temperature, sparking more fires in dry vegetation.

Changes in Vegetation

Climate change alters ecosystems, promoting the growth of flammable plants like dry grasses and shrubs. This shift increases the likelihood of fires starting and spreading.

Pests and Diseases

Warmer conditions allow pests (such as bark beetles) to thrive, killing millions of trees and leaving behind dry, dead wood that fuels wildfires. Insect infestations have devastated vast forests and turned them into fire-prone landscapes.

Human Activities and Climate Change

While climate change does not directly cause humans to start more wildfires, it exacerbates the risk by increasing the number of dry, fire-prone areas. In these conditions, activities like discarding cigarette butts or leaving campfires unattended become much riskier.

Carbon Emissions and a Vicious Cycle

Wildfires release massive amounts of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere, further intensifying the greenhouse effect. Each year, wildfires contribute 5 to 8 billion tonnes of CO2 globally, creating a feedback loop where climate change increases wildfires and wildfires accelerate climate change.

This dangerous cycle underscores the urgency of addressing both wildfire management and global efforts to mitigate climate change.

Devastating Wildfires in Los Angeles: A Catastrophic Impact on Southern California in 2025

Southern California is currently grappling with an unprecedented series of wildfires that have ravaged thousands of acres, destroyed over 1,300 structures, and forced the evacuation of over 179,000 residents.

The Palisades fire, the most destructive blaze, has scorched more than 17,000 acres in Pacific Palisades, while the Eaton fire in Altadena has consumed over 10,600 acres.

Wind-Fueled Destruction

Fueled by a severe windstorm, the wildfires spread rapidly, leaving a trail of destruction in their wake. Pacific Palisades and Altadena have been particularly affected, with iconic coastal neighborhoods and hillside communities suffering extensive damage.

The fires have also caused significant traffic congestion as evacuees have fled the area, often abandoning their vehicles to escape on foot.

Federal Support and Emergency Measures

In response to this crisis, President Biden has announced full federal disaster response funding for the next six months, covering debris removal, temporary shelters, and first responder costs.

The federal government has deployed 400 firefighters and 30 aircraft to aid the containment efforts. California Governor Gavin Newsom has also declared a state of emergency, emphasizing the extreme risk posed by the wind-driven flames.

Mental Health and Long-Term Recovery

Beyond the immediate physical damage, officials have highlighted the mental health strain on affected communities. Vice President Kamala Harris described the scene as "apocalyptic," emphasizing the emotional toll on residents who have lost their homes or been forced to evacuate.

Federal agencies including FEMA are working to provide essential supplies and support rebuilding efforts.

As wildfires continue to rage across Los Angeles County, communities face immense challenges in recovery and rebuilding. The collaborative efforts of state and federal agencies aim to mitigate the impact of one of California’s most expensive disasters in history.

The Link Between Amazon Deforestation and Wildfires in Other Regions

Deforestation in the Amazon rainforest is not only a local environmental crisis: its impacts ripple out across the globe, contributing to worsening wildfire conditions in distant regions, including California and parts of the western United States.

The Amazon plays a crucial role in regulating the Earth’s climate by acting as a massive carbon sink and a generator of atmospheric moisture. When this delicate balance is disrupted by deforestation, the effects can be felt thousands of miles away.

The Amazon's Role in Climate Regulation

The Amazon rainforest stores immense amounts of carbon in its vegetation and soil. This carbon is slowly released into the atmosphere when trees and plants die naturally. However, deforestation accelerates this process dramatically.

When large swaths of forest are cut down and burned, often to clear land for agriculture or cattle ranching, the stored carbon is released almost instantaneously in greenhouse gases, such as carbon dioxide and methane.

In 2021 alone, an estimated 75 million tons of carbon were released from the Amazon due to deforestation. This contributes directly to global warming, as higher concentrations of greenhouse gases trap more heat in the atmosphere.

This warming sets off a dangerous feedback loop that exacerbates drought conditions globally, including in regions like California, which already face prolonged dry seasons.

How the Amazon Regulates the Climate

As the Amazon is deforested, its ability to generate moisture and regulate rainfall is diminished. The rainforest produces massive amounts of water vapor through a process called evapotranspiration, in which water is released from trees into the atmosphere.

This vapor helps form rain clouds that not only sustain the forest itself but also influence weather patterns around the world.

Deforestation disrupts this process, leading to reduced rainfall both locally and globally. In areas like the western United States, where water scarcity is already a growing concern, the loss of this atmospheric moisture further intensifies drought conditions.

It is these drier conditions that create the perfect environment for wildfires to ignite and spread.

Forest Fragmentation and Edge Effects

Deforestation in the Amazon also leads to forest fragmentation, where smaller, isolated patches of trees are surrounded by open areas.

These fragmented forests are more susceptible to edge effects, where the edges of the forest dry out faster than the interior due to increased exposure to sunlight and wind. This drying effect makes the forest more flammable and increases the likelihood of wildfires.

The carbon released from these fires further contributes to climate change, which in turn fuels extreme weather events, including heat waves and prolonged dry spells in California and elsewhere.

The cycle becomes self-reinforcing: deforestation leads to fires, fires release carbon, and the resulting warming strengthens conditions for more fires globally.

The Role of Climate Change in Worsening the Problem

The climate crisis plays a significant role in amplifying the effects of Amazon deforestation. Rising global temperatures, partly driven by emissions from deforestation, create hotter, drier conditions worldwide.

In California, for instance, climate change has already extended the wildfire season, reduced snowpack levels, and caused drier vegetation—all prime conditions for devastating wildfires.

Why Action to Protect the Amazon Can’t Wait

The link between Amazon deforestation and wildfires in regions like California lays plain the urgent need for coordinated global action. While the Brazilian government has implemented some fire prevention strategies, these measures often fail to address the root causes of deforestation, such as illegal logging, agricultural expansion, and weak enforcement of environmental laws.

To break this destructive cycle, stronger and more strategic policies are needed. These should include:

●     Enhanced monitoring and enforcement: Technologies like artificial intelligence and satellite imagery can help predict and prevent deforestation and fires.

●     Global cooperation: Encouraging international efforts to protect the Amazon are critical because its preservation benefits the entire planet.

●     Restoration initiatives: Investing in reforestation programs can help recover degraded areas and restore the Amazon’s ability to act as a carbon sink and moisture generator.

By addressing deforestation in the Amazon, the global community can help mitigate its far-reaching impacts, including the intensification of wildfires in other parts of the world. Protecting the Amazon is not just a regional priority; it is a critical step toward safeguarding the planet's climate and reducing the risks of catastrophic wildfires worldwide.

Urgent Action Needed: Addressing the Wildfire and Climate Change Crisis

In conclusion, the connection between wildfires and climate change encapsulates a rapidly intensifying global crisis.

As rising temperatures and prolonged droughts fuel more frequent and severe wildfires, the destruction of ecosystems, loss of biodiversity, and release of greenhouse gases all contribute to exacerbating climate change.

Addressing this crisis requires urgent action, including mitigation efforts to combat climate change, improved fire management strategies, and global cooperation to protect vulnerable regions.
